REHAN  /  2026

STRYDE

Step Into The Game

A purpose-built premium sports theatre for urban India — designed to bring the feeling of being inside a stadium to every fan, at every budget.

Project Type Interior / Experience Design
02 — Brief

The Sphere cost $2.3 billion.
What does immersive sport look like when it's built for India?

Elsewhere in the world, sports viewing is becoming spectacle at an unprecedented scale. The MSG Sphere in Las Vegas. Dome theatres in Tokyo and London. Venues where the experience of watching a game rivals the game itself.

None of that exists in India. And in its current form, none of it could — the budgets don't translate, the formats don't fit, and the audiences are different in ways that matter.

STRYDE asks a different question: what does world-class sports viewing look like when it's designed for Indian cities, Indian fans, and Indian budgets?

Not a scaled-down Sphere. Something built from scratch — accessible enough that a student can afford a ticket, impressive enough that walking in stops you for a moment.

Walk through any urban neighbourhood during an IPL final or a FIFA World Cup match and you'll find the same scene: crowds gathered around a flickering screen mounted on a wall, people standing for hours, bad audio, worse sightlines, zero comfort. The energy is real. The conditions are not.

It works. Barely. And only because there's nothing better. No one is choosing this — they're tolerating it because the alternative is watching alone.

The problem isn't passion. Indian fans have that in abundance — 678 million of them, following cricket, football, kabaddi, NBA, Formula 1, and more. They stay up until 3am for matches. They crowd pavements for finals. They care deeply. The problem is infrastructure. Public screenings are temporary by design. Sports bars are built for drinking, not watching. Cinema halls feel too formal for the reactive energy sport actually needs.

The result is a country with one of the world's largest sports audiences and almost nowhere to watch together, properly.

A fan watching alone at home already has better picture and audio than anything available in public. If a space can't beat that experience, it has no reason to exist.

— The design insight that drove STRYDE

05 — Secondary Research

What the data says about India's sports audience

Ormax Media, 2024
678M

Indians are active sports followers — the largest sports audience base in the world after China. Cricket leads at 612M, followed by Football at 305M and Kabaddi at 280M.

Deloitte–Google, 2024
90%

Of Indian sports fans now follow multiple sports. This is the strongest era of multi-sport consumption India has ever seen — and no dedicated viewing space serves all of them.

Deloitte–Google, 2024
$130B

Projected value of India's sports industry by 2030, up from ~$2B today. The audience is scaling fast. The infrastructure to support them is not keeping pace.

Upstox Originals, 2025
93%

Of Gen Z fans use a second screen while watching sport. The home experience is fractured and distracted. A purpose-built theatre removes that friction — one screen, all attention.

National Sports Governance Act Analysis, 2025
"Treated Abysmally"

Indian sports fans have historically faced inadequate stadium facilities, deficient crowd management and poor event information — documented in legal policy analysis as a systemic gap in the ecosystem.

Sport Business / Long Game, 2023
"Best viewed together"

Sport is still best experienced as a communal act — families in living rooms, villages around big screens. The collective is the point. Individual home viewing is a compromise, not a preference.

Taken together, these data points describe a single condition: India has one of the world's largest, most passionate, and most diversified sports audiences — and almost zero infrastructure designed to serve them at scale. The gap isn't in demand. It's entirely in supply.

06 — Social Listening

What fans are actually saying

Paraphrased sentiment from Reddit, Twitter/X, and online forums — not direct quotes. Method: social listening.

r/IndiaCricket  /  recurring theme

"Standing for three hours at a street screening with no sightlines while the sound cuts out every five minutes isn't watching the match — it's surviving it."

A recurring frustration across Indian sports forums during major tournaments — the collective energy of outdoor screenings is real, but the physical experience consistently lets fans down.

Twitter/X  /  FIFA World Cup 2022

"I have excellent internet. My TV is good. The match is still unwatchable. At this point just give me a decent theatre to go to."

Widely expressed during the FIFA 2022 broadcast failures on JioCinema — Indian fans actively sought out an alternative communal space when home viewing broke down.

Online forums  /  NBA fans India

"There's nowhere to watch the game with people who actually care about it. Sports bars have one screen tucked behind a pillar and the cricket is always on anyway."

NBA and global sports fans in Indian cities consistently report the absence of any dedicated communal space — they're invisible to existing venues, which still default to cricket.

Twitter/X  /  IPL season recurring

"The atmosphere at a street screening is unreal. I just wish I didn't have to sit on the ground and couldn't see half the screen."

The tension at the heart of STRYDE's brief — fans genuinely love the communal energy of public screenings, but the physical conditions make them unsustainable as a regular experience.

Research Note

The above represents paraphrased sentiment patterns identified through secondary social listening — aggregated themes from r/IndiaCricket, r/nba, Twitter/X sports discussions, and online forums during major sporting events between 2022–2024. These are not direct quotes and have not been verified through primary user interviews. A future iteration of this research would benefit from structured interviews and usability testing with actual sports fans.

07 — Proto-Persona

Who we designed for

Built from secondary research + contextual observation. Not interview-validated — this is a design hypothesis.

Zenunni

22  /  Kochi

Occupation Engineering student
Sports followed Cricket, NBA, F1
Current setup Hostel room, laptop
Budget ₹150–300 per outing
The Situation

Zenunni follows cricket obsessively, but his real passion is the NBA — he stays up until 3am to watch Lakers games on a 13-inch laptop screen with earphones in. He's tried watching in sports bars but cricket is always on, the audio is wrong, and finding a seat where you can actually see the screen is luck. When the IPL final came around, he went to a street screening with friends — and loved the energy, hated everything else. He stood for four hours, couldn't hear the commentary, and left with a sore back.

Goals
  • Watch matches with people who actually care
  • A big screen he can actually see from his seat
  • Food and a comfortable place to sit
  • Something he can afford on a student budget
Frustrations
  • Every public space defaults to cricket only
  • Street screenings are exciting but physically awful
  • Sports bars feel too expensive for what you get
  • Watching alone at home feels like missing out
How STRYDE serves Zenunni

Multi-sport programming means the NBA gets its own screen time — not squeezed out by cricket. Tiered sofa seating means he can actually sit. Accessible ticket pricing means it fits a student budget. And the tunnel entrance means walking in actually feels like something — not just finding a chair in a bar.

08 — Design Principles

Four rules that shaped every decision

01
Immersion Over Spectacle

The goal isn't to impress with scale — it's to dissolve the distance between fan and game. Every material, every light source, every sightline should make the screen feel like a window, not a screen.

02
Every Sport, Every Fan

India watches cricket. It also watches NBA at 2am, Formula 1 on Sunday mornings, and Kabaddi finals nobody has a big enough screen for. STRYDE is not a cricket venue. It's built for all of them.

03
The Journey Matters

The experience begins before the match. The entrance tunnel, the lobby, the athlete portraits — these moments build anticipation. Walking into STRYDE should feel like walking into an arena.

04
Indian Scale, World Standard

Immersion comes from design intelligence, not construction budgets. STRYDE is not a compromised Sphere — it's a different answer to the same question, built for a different economy and a different audience.

09 — Design Process

From rough wall plan to rendered space

10 — The Experience

A journey through five zones

The design is best understood as a sequence — not a floor plan. From the moment you arrive to the moment the match starts, every zone builds on the last.

EXTERIOR / ARRIVAL Page 14 or 15 — facade render
01
Zone One — Arrival

The Facade

The building announces itself without apology. A stark monolithic volume in raw concrete, a black steel canopy extending outward, and the STRYDE name in backlit letters large enough to read from across the street.

The exterior is deliberately restrained — dark, bold, and still. The energy is being saved for inside. The glass frontage of the lobby offers a glimpse of what's in there: warm light, copper columns, movement.

LOBBY / STEP INTO THE GAME Page 16 — lobby interior with bubble lights
02
Zone Two — Lobby

Step Into The Game

The lobby is the decompression zone between the city and the stadium. Warm copper columns rise from dark bases. Suspended bubble lights cluster overhead. The phrase "Step Into The Game" runs along the wall in bold lettering.

A tower of backlit athlete portraits — presented in stacked cube frames — stands at the far end. These aren't decorative. They're there to remind you of the scale of what sport actually is, and what you're here to be part of.

FOOD ZONE Pages 18–19 — Popcorn / Fried Express counters
03
Zone Three — Food Zone

Fried Express

The food zone runs along the side wall — a series of colour-coded counters for popcorn, beverages, snacks, and hot food. Each stall has its own identity: deep red, forest green, bright yellow, warm copper cladding.

The colours are intentional — they break the monochrome palette of the rest of the space, giving the food zone its own energy. This is where you linger before the match. A chevron floor pattern runs beneath the counters, echoing the sports-direction motif used elsewhere in the building.

THE PLAYER TUNNEL Page 20 — red-lit corridor render
04
Zone Four — The Tunnel

The Ritual

This is the moment the design was built around. A long corridor, walls lined in dark woven mesh, edges glowing in deep red neon. A single pendant light hangs at the far end. No signage. No distraction.

You walk through it the way a player walks out of a changing room. The tension is physical. By the time you reach the theatre doors, you're already in match mode. This is not a corridor. It's a ritual — the design's clearest statement of intent.

THE THEATRE Pages 21–23 — tiered seating, screen render
05
Zone Five — The Theatre

The Main Event

Tiered seating rises toward the back — soft black lounge seats arranged in rows, copper handrails running the length of each tier. Red-edged steps mark the aisles. Wall-mounted speakers sit flush in acoustic panelling that lines both side walls in alternating dark stripes.

The screen is large enough to fill your peripheral vision from the front row. Everything in the space — the rake of the floor, the angle of the seats, the position of the speakers — was calculated to put the screen at the centre of every sensory experience in the room.

This is what the whole building is for.

11 — Design Details

The decisions that made the difference

COPPER COLUMNS

Copper Columns

Warm against the monochrome palette. Structural but decorative — they bring a sense of premium materiality without breaking the dark atmosphere.

CHEVRON FLOOR

Chevron Floor

A directionality motif — pointing inward, toward the theatre. The floor guides before signage does. Used at the entrance and carried through the food zone.

ATHLETE CUBES

Athlete Portrait Cubes

Stacked backlit cube frames in the lobby. Black-and-white portraits — sport as history, not just entertainment. A quiet moment of reverence before the match.

Test Your Vertical

A wall-mounted vertical jump measurement installation near the restrooms. A small moment of play — a reminder that sport isn't just for watching.

12 — Reflection

What worked. What's next. What I'd do differently.

What worked

The Tunnel

The player entrance corridor became the emotional centre of the project. It's the moment that most clearly communicates STRYDE's intent — that this is about feeling, not just watching. It's the detail I'd keep in every future version.

What worked

The Scale Argument

Framing STRYDE against global venues like the Sphere gave the project a clear thesis. It's not just a nicer sports bar — it's a specific answer to a specific gap between what India's sports audience deserves and what currently exists.

What's next

User Testing the Flow

The spatial sequence — lobby to food zone to tunnel to theatre — is intuitive on paper. A real test would be watching people navigate it without wayfinding, and seeing where the design breaks down. That's the next step.

What's next

Multi-Screen Programming

If multiple matches happen simultaneously, how does STRYDE handle it? A split-screen system or a secondary lounge zone for overflow — that programming question would shape the next version of the floor plan significantly.

What I'd change

Deeper User Research

The project was driven by observation and intuition. A structured survey of how Indian sports fans actually behave during public screenings — what they tolerate, what breaks the experience — would have sharpened every decision.

What I'd change

Acoustic Design

The acoustic panelling is visually resolved but acoustically unverified. In the next iteration, the theatre's sound design — speaker placement, panel angles, reverb control — would be modelled properly, not assumed.
